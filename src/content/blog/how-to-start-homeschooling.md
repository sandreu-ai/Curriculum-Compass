Starting to homeschool can feel like standing at the edge of a cliff. There's the legal side ("Do I need to file something?"), the curriculum side ("Which of the 40+ options is right for us?"), and the emotional side ("Am I actually capable of teaching my own child?").

Take a breath. Thousands of families start homeschooling every year with no teaching background. You can absolutely do this. Here's the order we recommend — and the traps most new families fall into.

## Step 1: Check your state's homeschool law *before* anything else

Homeschool law in the U.S. varies wildly by state. Some states require nothing more than pulling your child out of public school. Others require you to file an annual notice of intent, keep a portfolio, and have your child assessed each year.

The single most important thing you can do in your first week is read your state's actual requirements. Not a forum post about them — the real rules.

Our [50-state directory](/directory#state-laws) summarizes notice requirements, portfolio rules, and assessment obligations with links to each state's official page. Bookmark your state's row and treat it as a checklist.

## Step 2: Figure out your "why"

Families homeschool for wildly different reasons — and the reason shapes everything that follows.

- **Academic fit:** Your child is bored, behind, or thriving in ways the school can't accommodate.
- **Values:** You want faith, philosophy, or worldview woven through the day instead of siloed into one subject.
- **Flexibility:** You travel, have a non-standard work schedule, or want to follow your child's interests deeper than a school day allows.
- **Special needs:** Your child has a learning difference — dyslexia, ADHD, gifted, twice-exceptional — and a one-size-fits-all pace isn't working.

Write your "why" down on an index card. When curriculum shopping gets overwhelming in month three (and it will), that card tells you which features are non-negotiable and which are just shiny.

## Step 3: File whatever your state requires — then stop worrying about paperwork

If your state requires notice, submit it. If it doesn't, don't invent work for yourself. Most new families either panic and over-document or ignore the law and get surprised later. Neither serves you.

One folder. Physical or digital. In it: your state's requirement page printed out, your filed notice (if applicable), a blank attendance log, and any required forms for the year. That's your compliance file. Touch it when you have to; otherwise leave it alone.

## Step 4: Pick a *starting* curriculum — not your forever curriculum

This is where most families stall for weeks. Here's the unlock: your first curriculum doesn't have to be right. It just has to be good enough to start.

Homeschooling is iterative. Almost every experienced homeschool mom you'll meet changed curriculum in year one. The point of the first choice is to get moving, observe how your child learns, and calibrate from there.

A few honest truths about picking:

- **Don't buy every subject at once.** Start with math and language arts. Add science, history, and electives over the first two months once you have a rhythm.
- **Boxed (all-in-one) curricula are not a failure.** They're a gift for beginners. You can always go à la carte in year two.
- **Your child's learning style matters more than the curriculum's reputation.** A curriculum that's beloved in Facebook groups can be wrong for your kid.
- **Budget honestly.** Homeschool curriculum ranges from free to $2,000+ per child per year. There are excellent options at every price point.

## Step 5: Set up a space — a small one

You do not need a dedicated schoolroom with alphabet posters. Plenty of homeschool families do everything at the kitchen table. What you actually need:

- A spot where materials live so you're not hunting for the math book every morning.
- A basket or shelf per child.
- A wall calendar or printed weekly plan somewhere visible.
- Good lighting and a comfortable chair.

That's it. Pinterest homeschool rooms are nice, not necessary.

## Step 6: Start short

Here's the number-one mistake new homeschool families make: trying to recreate a full 7-hour school day. Don't.

A well-run homeschool day for an elementary-age child is typically **2–3 hours of focused work**. Middle school is 3–4 hours. High school can be 4–6. That is not a typo. One-on-one instruction is dramatically more efficient than classroom instruction, and the "extra" hours public school requires are structural, not academic.

In your first month, aim for:

- **Week 1–2:** Math and reading only. 30–45 minutes total.
- **Week 3–4:** Add writing and one content subject (science or history). 60–90 minutes.
- **Month 2+:** Round out to a normal rhythm, usually 2–3 hours.

If you're hitting resistance, shorter is almost always the answer. A 20-minute math lesson done cheerfully every day crushes a 60-minute lesson done once and then abandoned in frustration.

## Step 7: Build your community

Homeschooling alone is the fastest path to burnout. In the first month:

- Find **one** local co-op, park day, or homeschool group. Just one.
- Follow 2–3 thoughtful homeschool voices online — not 50.
- Identify **one** experienced homeschool mom you can text when you have a "is this normal?" question.

You are not trying to build the perfect village on day one. You're trying to avoid isolation.

## The first-year reality check

Your first year will include a week where you cry, a week where you feel like a homeschool genius, and at least one subject you bought, used twice, and quietly set aside. All of that is completely normal. The families who thrive are the ones who adjust quickly and don't treat every wobble as proof they're failing.

You're not running a school. You're raising a specific child. You get to match the tools to that child instead of the other way around. That is the whole point.
